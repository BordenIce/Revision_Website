The purpose of analysis in system development is to thoroughly understand and define the requirements of a system before the design and implementation stages begin. It involves identifying the needs of the users, determining the system's objectives, and specifying the functionalities that the system must deliver. Analysis serves as the foundation for building a system that meets user expectations and business goals. By conducting a detailed analysis, developers can minimize the risks of misunderstandings and errors that might arise later in the project, ensuring that the system is both effective and efficient.

Functional requirements specify what the system should do, such as processing transactions, managing inventory, or handling reservations. For instance, in a stock control system, functional requirements might include tracking inventory levels and generating reorder alerts. In a payroll system, they might involve calculating employee salaries and deductions accurately.

Non-functional requirements define the system's attributes, such as performance, security, usability, and reliability. These are critical for ensuring that the system meets user expectations in terms of speed, security, and user experience. For example, a reservation system might require a non-functional requirement of being able to handle a large number of concurrent users without performance degradation.

Intervews

Intervews involve direct communication with stakeholders to gather detailed insights about their needs and expectations. This method is highly effective for understanding complex requirements and obtaining qualitative data

Questionnaires

Questionnaires allow for the collection of information from a large number of users quickly. They are useful for gathering quantitative data and identifying common trends or opinions among users.

Observations

Observation involves watching how users interact with the current system or perform their tasks. This technique is valuable for uncovering issues that users may not be able to articulate or might overlook.

Document Sampling

Document Sampling entails reviewing existing documentation, such as reports, forms, and manuals, to understand the current processes and data flows. It helps in identifying requirements that are already documented and ensures that nothing is missed.

Purpose of a Data Flow Diagram (DFD)

A Data Flow Diagram (DFD) visually represents how data moves through a system, showing processes, data stores, and the flow between them. Its purpose is to give a clear overview of the system's functionality and interactions, helping both technical and non-technical stakeholders understand how the system works. DFDs are useful in identifying potential issues and ensuring all requirements are met, especially in complex systems, by breaking down processes into manageable parts for easier analysis and design.

Producing Context and Level 1 DFDs

Creating a context DFD maps the system's boundaries and interactions with external entities, like users or other systems. This high-level diagram offers an overview of the system. A Level 1 DFD breaks down the main process into sub-processes, showing how data moves between them and external entities. It helps in understanding the system's internal workings, essential for designing its architecture. For example, in a payroll system, a Level 1 DFD might show processes like "Calculate Pay," "Generate Payslip," and "Update Employee Records" with the data flows between them.
